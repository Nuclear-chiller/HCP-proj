Deep Dive: HCP Physics

01. The ABAB Stacking Pattern

The defining characteristic of Hexagonal Close Packing is its alternating layer sequence. Unlike the Cubic Close Packing (ABCABC) found in Copper or Silver, HCP repeats every two layers.

  • Layer A: Spheres are packed in a hexagonal array.
  • Layer B: Spheres rest in the triangular voids of Layer A.
  • Layer A (Top): The third layer is placed exactly directly above the spheres of the first layer, eclipsing them completely.

02. Coordination Number

Every atom in an HCP lattice touches 12 other atoms:

  • 6 atoms in the same plane (surrounding it).
  • 3 atoms in the plane above.
  • 3 atoms in the plane below.

03. Interstitial Voids

Even with 74% efficiency, 26% of the space is empty. These spaces are classified as voids:

  • Tetrahedral Voids: Surrounded by 4 spheres.
  • Octahedral Voids: Surrounded by 6 spheres.

04. The Unit Cell Geometry

The primitive unit cell is a hexagonal prism. While the larger hexagonal prism shows the symmetry better, the primitive cell contains the fundamental repeating unit.

Corner Atoms Each corner contributes 1/6 to the cell.
Face Atoms Face centers contribute 1/2 to the cell.
Center Atoms 3 atoms are fully inside the cell.

Total Effective Atoms (Z) = 6

05. Real World Metals

HCP is common in nature, particularly in metals that are brittle at room temperature due to limited slip systems.

Magnesium (Mg) Zinc (Zn) Titanium (Ti) Cadmium (Cd) Cobalt (Co)

06. The c/a Ratio

This ratio defines the height of the hexagonal prism (c) relative to its base edge (a).

Ideal Ratio: 1.633 (Derived geometrically).

Real Ratio: Most metals deviate slightly from this. For example, Zinc has a ratio of ~1.85 (elongated), while Titanium is ~1.58 (squashed), affecting their physical ductility.
